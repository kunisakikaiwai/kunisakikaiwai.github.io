Before there was a country called Japan, there were seas.

Far from separating people, the seas of East Asia connected them. The Seto Inland Sea, in particular, was one of the great maritime highways of the ancient world, carrying people, ideas, technologies, and beliefs from one shore to another.

People had already lived on the Kunisaki Peninsula for thousands of years. By the Jomon period, its mild climate, rich forests, abundant fishing grounds, and nearby sources of obsidian made it an attractive place to settle.

But the greatest transformation was still to come.

Beginning around the first millennium BCE, migrants from the Korean Peninsula brought with them rice cultivation, ironworking, new agricultural knowledge, and a more organized way of living in larger communities.

These were more than new technologies. \
Rice farming required people to work together. \
Iron tools changed agriculture and craftsmanship. \
Knowledge of the seasons made harvests more predictable. \
Gradually, these innovations reshaped the way people lived, cooperated, and governed themselves.

Across the Japanese archipelago, different regional cultures embraced these new ideas in different ways. Rather than becoming identical, each region blended the newcomers' knowledge with its own traditions, creating distinctive local cultures.

One of those cultures flourished in what was later known as **Toyo Province**, including present-day Usa and the Kunisaki Peninsula.

Its location was ideal. Facing both the Seto Inland Sea and the Bungo Channel, Toyo stood at the crossroads of maritime trade and cultural exchange. Local leaders maintained connections with neighboring regions while preserving their own traditions and sacred places.

Long before **Usa Shrine** existed, one mountain already stood at the spiritual center of the region. That mountain was **Mount Omoto**, where the deity later enshrined at Usa Shrine is believed to have first been worshipped. Sacred mountains like this were among the earliest places where people sought the presence of the divine, centuries before large shrine buildings became common.

Meanwhile, exchanges across the sea became increasingly dynamic. The Korean kingdoms, the Chinese mainland, and the peoples of the Japanese archipelago were no longer connected by one-way transfers of technology. Merchants, diplomats, craftsmen, monks, and migrants all carried their own knowledge, beliefs, and customs across the Yellow Sea and the East China Sea.

Japan was becoming part of a much larger East Asian world.

Among the many regional powers of the archipelago, the rulers of Yamato became especially active in building diplomatic ties with the kingdoms of the Korean Peninsula. Through these relationships, they learned new ideas about government, administration, writing, and statecraft.

Over time, the Yamato court would emerge as the political center of Japan. Yet this did not erase the cultures that had developed elsewhere. The traditions of Izumo, Hyuga, Toyo, and many other regions continued to shape the country's spiritual landscape. The Kunisaki Peninsula would follow its own path as well.

Rooted in the ancient beliefs of Toyo, enriched by centuries of exchange across the sea, this small peninsula was quietly preparing for the remarkable religious world that would later emerge here.

The sea had brought more than people. It had brought the ideas that would change history.
