By the seventh century, the rulers of Yamato faced a difficult question. How could they govern an archipelago filled with powerful regional leaders, each with its own traditions, beliefs, and political influence?

Part of the answer arrived through an unexpected tragedy.

For centuries, Japan had maintained close ties with the Korean kingdom of **Baekje**. Craftsmen, monks, scholars, and skilled artisans had long crossed the sea, bringing with them new ideas and technologies.

Then, in 660 CE, Baekje fell. Many members of its royal family, officials, scholars, and craftsmen fled to Japan, bringing with them not only their skills but also firsthand knowledge of one of East Asia's most advanced civilizations.

Only a few years later, Yamato attempted to restore its ally by sending a large military force to the Korean Peninsula.

The campaign ended in disaster. In 663, Yamato was defeated by the combined forces of Tang China and Silla in what is now known as the **Battle of Baekgang**.

The defeat was a turning point. Rather than turning inward, the Yamato court began asking a different question:

*"What can we learn from the civilizations that defeated us?"*

The answer was ambitious. Yamato welcomed the refugees from Baekje and eagerly adopted many elements of continental civilization—Buddhism, writing, systems of law, and new models of government inspired by China.

Yet Japan did not simply copy what it imported. The legal system known today as the **Ritsuryō Code** was based on Chinese models, but it was adapted to fit Japanese realities. China's imperial government sought direct control over every part of its territory.

Japan took a different approach. Rather than replacing powerful local families, Yamato recruited them. Regional leaders became district governors, responsible for maintaining official records, collecting taxes, and administering their own communities on behalf of the central government.

In return, they retained much of their local authority and prestige. For many local leaders, this was a practical choice. Joining the new state offered military protection, political stability, and recognition, while allowing them to preserve the traditions that had long defined their communities.

Instead of ruling entirely through force, Yamato built alliances. \
Instead of destroying regional cultures, it found a place for them within a larger political system. \
This flexibility became one of the foundations of the early Japanese state.

As the authority of Yamato expanded, Japan gradually became more unified. Yet it never became culturally uniform. Across the archipelago, ancient regional traditions continued to flourish beneath the new political framework.

One of the strongest of these regional cultures lay in **Toyo Province**, on the border of present-day Oita and Fukuoka. Here, local beliefs remained vibrant even as the new state took shape. In the centuries that followed, those traditions would give rise to **Usa Shrine**—and eventually to the remarkable religious world of **Rokugo Manzan**.

Japan had become one country. But it remained a country of many worlds.
