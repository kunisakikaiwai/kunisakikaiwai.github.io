---
title: "After the Fire"
order: 10
slug: "10-after-fire"
---

The fire did not end the story. Many temples had been destroyed. The great religious network that had once embraced the entire peninsula had been broken.

Yet Rokugo Manzan itself did not disappear. Mountain ascetics continued their training. Monks remained where they could. Villagers quietly cared for the temples that had survived. Even when it could no longer be seen from afar, the flame of faith continued to burn.

Then came a different age.

The long peace of the Edo period transformed Japan. \
Agriculture became more productive. \
Coastal trade flourished. \
Villages grew more prosperous.

For the first time in generations, people had enough stability to think not only about survival, but about rebuilding. This time, the temples were not restored by great landed estates or powerful religious institutions.

They were rebuilt by local communities. \
Village leaders organized the work. \
Religious associations known as **kō** pooled money, labor, and materials. \
Carpenters rebuilt temple halls. \
Stonemasons repaired stairways. \
Bridges crossed mountain streams once again. \
One stone at a time, the landscape came back to life.

The temples also changed. Instead of serving a vast medieval religious network supported by landed estates, they became places where local communities gathered, prayed, and celebrated together.

Pilgrimage itself became part of everyday life. Like the great pilgrimages to Ise or Kotohira, journeys to the temples of Kunisaki became both acts of devotion and opportunities to travel beyond one's own village. Groups of pilgrims climbed the mountains together. They visited temples, offered prayers, shared meals, and carried home sacred amulets and stories.

Faith became something that was experienced together.

The mountains welcomed new visitors as well. Temples such as **Futago-ji** and **Tennen-ji** gradually became easier to reach. Ancient places of ascetic training remained sacred, but paths were improved, halls rebuilt, and festivals opened to ordinary worshippers. What had once been hidden among the mountains became part of the shared life of the peninsula.

Even places once known only to mountain ascetics gained new meaning. The **Mumyō Bridge**, built high between narrow ridges, had long been a place of severe spiritual training. Over time, it also became a symbol of Rokugo Manzan itself. Visitors no longer saw only a bridge. They saw the determination of the people who had built it—and the faith that had kept the tradition alive.

The old system had not been restored. It had been reinvented. Once, the temples had been supported by medieval estates. Now they were sustained by villages, pilgrims, festivals, and the people who returned year after year.

The form had changed. The spirit had not.

Many of the landscapes visitors admire today were shaped during this period. The stone Buddhas are ancient. The mountains are timeless. But the pilgrimage paths, village traditions, festivals, and much of the living culture surrounding them grew into their present form during the centuries of rebuilding.

Rokugo Manzan survived because each generation found its own way to continue the story.

Today, flowers are still placed before the stone Buddhas. \
Temple roofs are still repaired. \
Pilgrims still climb the mountains.

Not because the Middle Ages somehow survived. But because generation after generation chose to keep this landscape alive.

When much of that world was lost, ordinary people quietly began again.

The story of Kunisaki was never written by emperors alone.

It was written by monks. \
By stonemasons. \
By farmers. \
By pilgrims. \
By countless people whose names are now forgotten.

Perhaps that is why this peninsula still feels alive today. Its story was never finished. It is simply waiting for the next traveler to continue reading it.
