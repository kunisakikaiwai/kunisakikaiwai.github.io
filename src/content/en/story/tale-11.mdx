History did not end with the Edo period. The Meiji Restoration brought yet another great transformation.

In an effort to modernize Japan, the government officially separated Shinto and Buddhism after more than a thousand years of coexistence.

Many temples disappeared. The great temple of **Miroku-ji**, which had stood beside Usa Shrine for centuries, was abolished. Mountain asceticism was officially prohibited.

Once again, Rokugo Manzan seemed to be losing the world that had shaped it.

Yet something remarkable happened. \
The institutions changed. \
The landscape remained. \
And the people did not forget.

Some villagers quietly protected Buddhist statues. \
Ancient festivals survived in new forms. \
Sacred paths continued to be walked. \
Little by little, the mountains welcomed people once again.

The Rokugo Manzan that visitors encounter today is not the same community that flourished in the Middle Ages. It is something different. It is the result of more than thirteen centuries of continual change.

Every generation inherited an old tradition. Every generation quietly reshaped it.

The mountains that once trained ascetic monks gradually became places where ordinary people came to pray. Then they became places where people also came to learn. Today, they are places where people come to walk.

Some visitors seek history. \
Others seek nature. \
Some come for architecture, festivals, or stone Buddhas. \
Others simply come for a quiet day in the mountains.

All of them are following paths that countless people have walked before.

The stone Buddhas remain. \
The mountain temples remain. \
Pilgrimage trails still cross the ridges. \
Festivals such as **Shujō Onie**, in which monks appear as demons, are still celebrated.

The story did not survive because every building survived. It survived because every generation chose to carry something forward.

That may be the true meaning of Rokugo Manzan. It has never been frozen in time. It has always been changing. And yet, somehow, it has always remained itself.

Millions of years ago, volcanoes created this peninsula. \
The sea connected it to the wider world. \
People built villages, shrines, temples, and communities upon its mountains.

Some were destroyed. Others were rebuilt. Every generation added another chapter.

Today, the story is still being written. Perhaps that is why Kunisaki feels different from so many historic places. It is not simply a place where history happened. It is a place where history is still quietly alive.

If you someday find yourself standing before a weathered stone Buddha... \
Crossing a narrow stone bridge high above a rocky valley... \
Or listening to temple bells echo across the mountains...

You are not simply visiting the past. You have become part of a story that began millions of years ago.

And now...

It is your turn to continue the journey.
