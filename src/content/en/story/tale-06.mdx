The idea did not appear overnight.

By this time, Buddhist temples and places of training had begun to spread around Usa and across the Kunisaki Peninsula. They did not begin as a single organization. Each temple had its own community, its own teachers, and its own traditions. But as their numbers grew, monks also began to emerge who could connect these scattered institutions and guide the people who depended on them.

And eventually, these Buddhist communities encountered another rising power.

**Usa Shrine.**

By then, Usa was no longer merely a local shrine. Through royal oracles, military campaigns, and its growing relationship with the Yamato court, Hachiman—the kami, or native Japanese deity, worshipped at Usa Shrine—had gained the trust of the central government.

Hachiman was increasingly understood not only as the protector of Usa, but as a guardian of the state itself. The name of Hachiman began to spread far beyond Kyushu.

But there was a problem. Buddhism had also become one of the great pillars of the state. In Nara, monks prayed for peace, temples performed rituals for the protection of the country, and Buddhist teachings had become deeply connected with imperial authority.

The gods of Usa and the Buddhas of the capital belonged to different religious worlds. At least in theory.

In everyday life, however, ordinary people had never seen such a sharp division. Those who had crossed the sea from the Korean Peninsula and the continent brought their own beliefs with them. Local people continued to honor the gods of the mountains, forests, fields, and sea.

Many did both. \
They visited shrines. \
They also visited temples. \
To them, there was no obvious contradiction.

The difficulty arose only when this lived reality had to be explained within the formal world of the court. If the kami and the Buddhas were rivals, how could both protect the same country?

A new answer gradually emerged. \
The kami were not enemies of Buddhism. \
They were protectors of the Buddhist teaching. \
They, too, could support the salvation of living beings.

Rather than arguing that the old gods should disappear, Buddhist thinkers gave them a place within a larger sacred order.

Japan did not ask, **"Which one is true?"**

It asked, **"How can they work together?"**

This idea transformed Usa. A Buddhist temple known as Miroku-ji was established as part of the Usa shrine-temple complex itself. The relationship between the shrine, nearby temples, and mountain practitioners grew steadily stronger.

For modern readers, the idea may seem unusual.

A shrine for the kami. \
A temple for the Buddhas. \
Side by side.

But in Usa, this was not seen as confusion. It was cooperation.

The shrine gained stronger ties to the court and to the Buddhist institutions that supported the state. The temples gained protection, prestige, and access to the growing influence of Hachiman. And ordinary people could continue to honor both traditions without being forced to choose between them.

This new religious partnership helped transform Hachiman into one of the most widely worshipped deities in Japan. Shrines dedicated to Hachiman eventually spread across the country. In later centuries, warriors would also adopt Hachiman as a protector, giving rise to his famous image as a god of war.

But that came later. At this moment, something more fundamental was taking shape.

The old gods had not been defeated. The new religion had not simply replaced them. Instead, both were being woven into a shared world.

This way of thinking would become known as **shinbutsu shūgō**—the blending of kami and Buddhas. And in Kunisaki, it would soon give rise to something even larger.

A powerful shrine stood in Usa. Temples and mountain practitioners were spreading across the peninsula.

Now they needed a way to belong to one another.

That shared world would become **Rokugo Manzan**.
