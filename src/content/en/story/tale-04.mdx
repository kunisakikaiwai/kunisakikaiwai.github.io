History does not always unfold as governments intend.

As Yamato expanded its authority across Japan, one might expect local communities to lose their independence and become increasingly controlled by the central government.

Instead, something rather unexpected happened.

For generations, people had already been farming the land, managing irrigation, maintaining villages, and worshipping their local deities. Regional leaders had long served as the organizers of both agriculture and community life.

The new Ritsuryō government provided a common legal framework, and in broad fertile plains, where disputes over land and water were frequent, that brought obvious advantages. But much of Japan was not made up of broad plains. It was a land of mountains, valleys, and peninsulas.

In places like the Kunisaki Peninsula, each valley already functioned as a self-contained community. People knew their own fields, managed their own irrigation systems, and relied on local cooperation to survive. Here, distant officials from the capital could never understand the land as well as the people who lived there.

Gradually, another system emerged. Rather than resisting the central government, local leaders chose to work with it. They donated land—at least on paper—to influential aristocrats, great temples, or major shrines connected to the imperial court. In return, those powerful institutions offered political protection.

The arrangement benefited everyone. \
The court strengthened its influence across the provinces. \
Powerful temples and shrines gained new sources of income. \
And local families continued managing the land much as they always had.

These landed estates, known as **shōen**, are sometimes described simply as a loophole in the Ritsuryō system. In reality, they were something more interesting.

They were a uniquely Japanese compromise.

Rather than replacing local society, the new state learned to work through it. Instead of weakening regional communities, the system often made them more prosperous. That prosperity would have consequences far beyond agriculture.

Religious institutions also became major landholders, receiving donations from communities throughout the country.

With growing estates came growing wealth. \
With growing wealth came growing influence. \
Few places were better positioned to benefit from this new world than **Usa Shrine**.

To the west stretched the fertile plains of Toyo, capable of producing abundant rice. To the east lay the deeply folded valleys of the Kunisaki Peninsula, where independent mountain communities had flourished for centuries.

Usa stood between these two worlds. Supported by both the agricultural wealth of the plains and the spiritual traditions of the mountains, the shrine grew into one of the most influential religious centers in Japan.

Ironically, a system designed to strengthen the authority of the central government also strengthened the regions themselves.

And among those regions, few would prove more remarkable than Kunisaki.

A powerful shrine was rising in Usa. Soon, another force would begin to rise beside it.

The monks had arrived.
