Nothing lasts forever.

For centuries, Usa Shrine and Rokugo Manzan had built a remarkable world together.

Faith supported the economy. \
The economy supported the temples. \
The temples supported the people. \
The people cared for the mountains. \
Everything depended upon everything else.

It was a world of balance.

But history rarely stands still.

As Japan entered the age of the samurai, political power gradually shifted away from the old aristocracy and toward military rulers. Usa Shrine adapted remarkably well. The shrine increasingly emphasized **Hachiman** not only as the protector of the nation but also as the guardian of warriors.

The first shogun, **Minamoto no Yoritomo**, became one of Hachiman's most devoted supporters, and the founding of **Tsurugaoka Hachimangū** in Kamakura helped spread Hachiman worship throughout Japan.

The shrine had successfully adapted to a changing age. Not everyone could.

The prosperity of Rokugo Manzan had been built upon a network of temples, landed estates, and communities that enjoyed considerable independence. For generations, this decentralized system had worked remarkably well.

But the Sengoku period demanded something different. Across Japan, powerful warlords sought to bring land, taxation, military force, and political authority under their direct control. Independent religious institutions increasingly came to be seen as obstacles to that goal.

The destruction of **Enryaku-ji** by **Oda Nobunaga** and the long conflict with the **Ishiyama Hongan-ji** were among the most famous examples. The same transformation eventually reached distant Bungo—the land of Kunisaki itself.

In Bungo, that transformation had a name: **Ōtomo Sōrin**.

Today, Sōrin is often remembered as the Christian daimyō who destroyed Buddhist temples. The reality was more complicated.

At first, he protected many temples, including those of Rokugo Manzan, recognizing their influence within his domain. But as his efforts to strengthen central authority continued, the enormous economic and political power of the temple estates became increasingly difficult to tolerate.

Christianity may have influenced some of his later decisions. Yet the deeper conflict was political. The old religious network no longer fit the new age.

Many temples were destroyed.

Imagine the scene.

Wooden halls that had stood for centuries. \
Libraries filled with Buddhist scriptures. \
Living quarters for monks. \
The sound of bells echoing through the valleys.

Then smoke. \
Flames climbing the mountainside. \
Roofs collapsing. \
Silence.

Traditions preserved at temples such as **Sendō-ji** speak of magnificent buildings consumed by fire.

Other places tell different stories. At **Iwato-ji**, monks are said to have retreated to nearby mountain fortifications and resisted. At **Tennen-ji**, some remote training sites hidden deep in the mountains survived, later becoming the foundation for rebuilding.

Yet even without war, Rokugo Manzan would have faced profound challenges. Its greatest strength had always been the economic system that sustained it. As the medieval estate system declined and military rulers consolidated direct control over the countryside, maintaining such a vast religious network became increasingly difficult.

The age that had created Rokugo Manzan was disappearing. \
The wooden halls burned. \
The economic network was broken. \
The old order came to an end.

But not everything was lost.

Fire could consume timber. It could not consume stone.

The stone stairways remained. \
The stone Buddhas remained. \
The cliffs where generations of monks had prayed remained. \
And so did the people who remembered.

Rokugo Manzan had lost its world. It had not lost its soul.

From those ashes, a quieter chapter of its story would begin.
