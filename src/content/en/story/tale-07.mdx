Rokugo Manzan did not suddenly appear. Like everything else in the story of Kunisaki, it grew gradually.

By this time, temples and mountain training sites were scattered across the peninsula. They shared many beliefs and practices, but each still functioned independently.

What transformed them into a single community?

The answer begins at **Miroku-ji**, the Buddhist temple attached to Usa Shrine. While Usa Shrine served the kami, Miroku-ji carried out Buddhist rituals. Through it, monks and temples across the Kunisaki Peninsula maintained loose connections, sharing teachings, rituals, and pilgrimage routes.

They already formed a network. They simply did not yet think of themselves as one community.

At about the same time, Buddhism itself was changing. In the Nara period (eighth century), Buddhism had largely served the state. But during the early Heian period (ninth century), new schools of Buddhism began looking toward the mountains instead of the court.

The most influential was **Tendai Buddhism**, founded by the monk **Saichō** on Mount Hiei near Kyoto. Tendai emphasized spiritual practice in the mountains. Yet for the monks of Kunisaki, this was not an entirely new idea. They had already been climbing ridges, meditating in caves, and training among forests and cliffs for generations.

Tendai did not invent mountain practice in Kunisaki. It gave those existing traditions a shared language, a broader vision, and a place within the wider world of Japanese Buddhism.

Gradually, the temples of the peninsula began reorganizing themselves. \
Monks traveled more frequently between valleys. \
Training methods became increasingly shared. \
Ceremonies grew more consistent.

The scattered temples slowly became a single religious community. The peninsula was eventually organized into six districts, known collectively as **Rokugō**, or "the Six Districts." Different temples assumed different responsibilities. Some guided religious practice, others trained monks, while smaller temples cared for the spiritual life of local villages.

But perhaps the most remarkable feature of Rokugo Manzan was not its structure. It was the way it governed itself.

Many religious institutions are built around a single powerful leader. Rokugo Manzan was not. Its temples worked through consultation rather than absolute authority. Representatives gathered, discussed important matters, and sought consensus.

It was less like a kingdom than a federation.

More importantly, Rokugo Manzan was never simply a collection of temples.

The temples were connected by mountain trails. \
The mountains overlooked farming villages. \
The villages supported the temples. \
The temples protected the sacred mountains. \
The sacred mountains gave meaning to the landscape. \
Everything belonged to the same living network.

For the monks, the entire peninsula became a place of practice. \
For the villagers, every valley became part of a sacred world. \
The mountains were no longer simply mountains. \
The villages were no longer simply villages. \
The forests, cliffs, shrines, temples, and pilgrimage paths together formed a single religious landscape.

Perhaps this is what makes Rokugo Manzan so unusual. It was never defined by one great temple. It was defined by an entire peninsula.

The stage that volcanoes had created millions of years earlier had finally found its purpose.

Kunisaki itself had become a sacred landscape.
