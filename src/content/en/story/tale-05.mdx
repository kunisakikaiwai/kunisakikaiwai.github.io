While Usa Shrine was steadily growing in influence, another community was quietly taking root across the Kunisaki Peninsula.

The monks had arrived.

In the capital of Nara, Buddhism had become an important institution of the state. Great temples were built with imperial support. Monks were officially recognized and regulated by the government. They studied Buddhist scriptures, performed state ceremonies, and prayed for the peace and stability of the nation.

Buddhism was not only a personal faith. It had become part of the machinery of government.

But the Buddhism that spread through Usa and Kunisaki was different.

Long before Buddhism became closely tied to the Japanese state, it had already been arriving in northern Kyushu little by little, carried across the sea by merchants, immigrants, craftsmen, and monks from the Korean Peninsula and the Asian continent.

Many of those monks settled within local communities. Their temples were not isolated centers of learning. They became places where ordinary people gathered—for worship, for advice, and for practical help in everyday life.

In Kunisaki, Buddhism grew alongside the region's older traditions rather than replacing them. It became a religion of the countryside.

The monks did far more than recite sutras. \
They comforted the sick. \
They prayed for rain during droughts. \
They offered hope during famines. \
Some passed on knowledge of medicine, engineering, or irrigation. \
Others simply became trusted members of the communities they served.

For the people of Kunisaki, Buddhism was not an abstract philosophy. It was something that helped them live.

One belief, in particular, found fertile ground here. It was devotion to **Maitreya**, the Buddha of the Future. According to Buddhist tradition, Maitreya will appear in a distant age to renew the world and bring salvation to all living beings. The belief had begun centuries earlier in India before spreading through China and the Korean Peninsula to Japan.

For people living in an age marked by war, disease, and famine, this promise carried enormous power. No matter how difficult life seemed today, a better world would one day arrive. Hope itself became an act of faith.

Around the same time, another tradition was quietly taking shape in the mountains of Kunisaki. The mountains were not yet filled with great temples. Most temples remained close to the villages where people lived.

The mountains served a different purpose. They became places of training. Monks climbed steep ridges, meditated in caves, prayed beside cliffs, and sought spiritual discipline in the natural world.

The mountains were not where they lived. They were where they transformed themselves.

Over generations, these practices gradually evolved into the mountain ascetic traditions that would later define **Rokugo Manzan**.

By now, Kunisaki had developed a form of Buddhism unlike that of the capital. It was rooted in local communities. It offered hope for ordinary people. And it found its sacred space not inside great cities, but among forests, cliffs, and mountain peaks.

Then something remarkable happened. This Buddhism encountered a land where ancient gods had already been worshipped for centuries.

Many cultures might have chosen between the old faith and the new one. Japan chose something entirely different. It did not choose between its gods and its Buddhas.

It imagined a world where both belonged together.
